# Finding Product–Market Fit

Product–market fit is the one thing that matters before anything else. Every other metric is a distraction if you don't have it, and a lever if you do.

## What PMF actually is

Not "some users like it." Not "we hit our MRR target."

PMF is when **pull exceeds push**. People seek you out. They complain when you're down. They tell their friends without being asked. You're no longer convincing customers — you're triaging demand.

If you're not sure whether you have it, you don't.

## Signals (in increasing order of strength)

1. Users come back on their own.
2. They refer others unprompted.
3. Churn is low and flat, not declining asymptotically.
4. Retention cohorts flatten at > 40% (rule of thumb, varies by segment).
5. You can stop marketing for a week and growth doesn't stop.
6. Sales cycles get shorter, not longer.
7. Competitors start copying you, not the other way around.

## Counter-signals (you don't have it)

- Every new user requires a demo call.
- Churn is "we'll fix it with retention features."
- You're talking about "the market" more than about users.
- Engineering velocity is high; usage is flat.
- Your best channel is you, personally, in DMs.

## Before PMF

Do less. Do it better. Specifically:

- **Narrow the target user.** "Early-stage technical founders building AI products" beats "developers."
- **Talk to 5 users a week.** Not surveys — real conversations. Watch them use it.
- **Ship weekly.** The only way to find what works is to keep testing what doesn't.
- **Kill features.** Every dead feature is friction for the user and cost for you.
- **Measure one thing.** Usually retention. Not revenue, not signups.

## The PMF illusion

Founders frequently mistake:

- **Novelty demand for PMF** — people try anything once.
- **Founder market fit for PMF** — you want this to work; users might not.
- **Revenue for PMF** — SDR-driven revenue can hide a bad product for a long time.

Test yourself: if you stopped all outbound for 30 days, would anything happen?

## After PMF

Different job. Now you scale the engine: hiring, infrastructure, paid channels, pricing. Most of what's written about "how to run a startup" is post-PMF advice misapplied to pre-PMF teams.

Don't confuse the phases.
