# Investor Updates That Actually Help

Monthly investor updates are a low-cost, high-leverage habit. Done well, they keep investors engaged (intros, hires, follow-on checks). Done poorly, they waste everyone's time. Most founders either skip them or over-engineer them.

## Why bother

- **Forcing function.** Writing clarifies thinking. You'll catch issues earlier.
- **Help, on demand.** Investors who know what you're working on can actually help you — intros, candidate referrals, customer leads.
- **Bridge to the next round.** Investors are tracking you. A steady drumbeat of progress, honestly reported, sets up the next raise.
- **Accountability.** Numbers you ship monthly are numbers you manage weekly.

## The shape

Short. Scannable. Ideally under 500 words. Every month, same day.

### Template

```
[Company] — Investor Update — [Month Year]

TL;DR
- [One sentence of the month's most important thing.]
- [One sentence of the most important worry.]

Metrics
- MRR: $X (+Y% MoM)
- Paying customers: N (+M)
- [One other metric that matters most right now]
- Cash: $X · Burn: $Y/mo · Runway: Z months

Wins
- [Specific, verifiable wins. Not adjectives.]

Lowlights
- [Real problems. Yes, really.]

Asks
- Intros to [specific kind of person].
- Looking to hire [role]. Referrals welcome.
- Feedback on [specific decision].

What's next
- [2–3 concrete goals for next month.]
```

## Principles

1. **Be honest about lowlights.** Investors who invest in you can tell when you're hiding things. Their BS detectors are calibrated against hundreds of updates.
2. **Ask specifically.** "Any intros would be great" gets you nothing. "Looking for design partners running 10–50 person engineering teams" gets you five emails.
3. **Same format every month.** Predictability means investors actually scan it.
4. **Lead with the worry, not the wins.** Investors skim. Put the thing they need to know first.
5. **Include runway every time.** It's the single number that determines how much help they can offer.

## What not to do

- **Long prose walls.** Nobody reads them.
- **Vague metrics.** "Growing nicely" is an anti-signal.
- **Skipping months when things are bad.** Those are the months investors most want to hear from you.
- **BCC everyone individually.** Use a mailing list (Mercury / Visible / Brex / plain email-group). Bonus: one canonical archive.
- **Asking for "general feedback."** Busy investors decline ambiguous requests.

## Cadence

Monthly is the default. Weekly is too much. Quarterly is too little.

A founder who sends 36 thoughtful updates over three years is a founder who is markedly easier to fund, hire for, and introduce.
